CING councillors are not bound by a rigid manifesto, but we do share a set of principles and priorities that guide our work on Cornwall Council. The positions set out below reflect the issues our communities raise with us most often and the direction we believe Cornwall needs to take.

## Housing

Cornwall's housing crisis affects every part of the county. Local people — particularly young families and key workers — are being priced out of the communities where they grew up, while the proliferation of second homes and short-term holiday lets reduces the supply of homes available for year-round residents.

We support measures that prioritise genuinely affordable housing for local people, including strengthened local connection requirements in planning policy and effective regulation of the short-term lettings market. New developments must be of high quality, appropriately scaled, and accompanied by the infrastructure — schools, surgeries, roads — that communities need to absorb growth sustainably.

We also believe that Cornwall Council itself must take a more active role as a housing enabler, working with housing associations and community land trusts to bring forward sites and deliver homes that remain affordable in perpetuity.

## Environment

Cornwall's natural environment is one of its greatest assets and its most important responsibilities. Our coastline, moorland, rivers, and hedgerows support rich biodiversity and underpin the tourism economy that so many livelihoods depend upon.

We advocate for robust protection of designated sites and habitats, and for Cornwall Council to lead by example in reducing its own carbon emissions and waste. Planning decisions must give proper weight to environmental impact, and developers should be held to the highest standards of ecological mitigation and net gain.

Equally, we recognise that environmental policy must work for rural communities. Farmers and land managers are stewards of the Cornish landscape, and any transition to more sustainable practices must be supported with practical advice, fair funding, and a recognition of the economic realities they face.

## Local Economy

A strong local economy is the foundation of thriving communities. Cornwall's economy is characterised by small and medium-sized enterprises, seasonal tourism, agriculture, fishing, and a growing creative and technology sector. Policy must reflect this diversity rather than applying one-size-fits-all approaches designed for urban centres.

We support investment in the infrastructure that enables businesses to grow — reliable broadband, adequate road maintenance, and accessible town centres. We believe Cornwall Council should use its procurement power to support local suppliers wherever possible, keeping public money circulating within the Cornish economy.

Skills and training are equally important. We want to see stronger partnerships between the Council, further education colleges, and local employers to ensure that young people in Cornwall have pathways to well-paid, fulfilling careers without having to leave the county.

## Transport

Reliable, affordable transport is essential to daily life in a large, rural county. Too many communities in Cornwall are poorly served by public transport, leaving residents dependent on private cars and isolated if they cannot drive.

We support a strategic approach to public transport that connects towns and villages with services people actually need — not just tourist routes that operate only in the summer months. Community transport schemes deserve greater recognition and funding, and the Council should work proactively with bus operators to protect and improve routes.

Road maintenance remains a persistent concern across Cornwall. We press for adequate funding to address the backlog of repairs and for a preventative approach that fixes problems before they become dangerous and costly. Investment in active travel infrastructure — safe cycling and walking routes — should complement, not replace, improvements to the road network.

## Governance

Cornwall Council spends hundreds of millions of pounds of public money each year, and residents have a right to know how those decisions are made. We believe in transparent, accountable governance where scrutiny is meaningful and dissenting voices are heard.

We advocate for stronger scrutiny committees with genuine power to examine executive decisions, call in proposals, and hold cabinet members to account. Council meetings should be accessible to the public, both in person and online, and agendas and reports should be published in good time.

As independent councillors, we also believe that the committee system of governance — where decisions are taken collectively by cross-party committees rather than concentrated in a small cabinet — merits serious consideration. Cornwall is too important, and its challenges too complex, to be governed by a handful of councillors behind closed doors.
